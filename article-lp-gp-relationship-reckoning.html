The LP-GP Relationship Is Facing a Reckoning

By Victoria Hernandez • March 11, 2026 • 8 min read

For the better part of a decade, limited partners allocated capital to venture funds with increasing enthusiasm and decreasing scrutiny. The asset class delivered extraordinary returns, unicorns multiplied, and the power dynamic shifted decisively toward general partners who could afford to be selective about their investor base. Those days are ending. As distributions have slowed to a trickle and paper valuations remain uncertain, LPs are reasserting their influence and demanding fundamental changes to how venture funds operate.

The most immediate pressure point is transparency. LPs have grown frustrated with quarterly letters that obscure more than they reveal, with valuation methodologies that seem designed to delay recognition of markdowns, and with communication practices that leave them guessing about the true health of portfolio companies. In response, sophisticated LPs are now requiring detailed portfolio company metrics as a condition of re-upping, pushing for standardized reporting formats, and in some cases demanding direct access to portfolio company management teams. GPs who resist these requests are finding themselves at a disadvantage when fundraising.

Fee structures are also under pressure. The traditional 2-and-20 model that served venture capital well for decades is being challenged by LPs who argue that it misaligns incentives and overcompensates fund managers during periods of rapid asset growth. Alternative structures are emerging: some LPs are negotiating for lower management fees that step down as funds grow larger, while others are pushing for hurdle rates that require GPs to generate meaningful returns before collecting carried interest. The most progressive LP-GP relationships are exploring hybrid structures that tie compensation more directly to distributions rather than paper gains.

The secondary market has become both a pressure relief valve and a new source of tension in LP-GP relationships. LPs sitting on illiquid positions have increasingly turned to secondary buyers to generate the distributions their own stakeholders require. But these transactions can create conflicts with GPs who prefer to control the timing and terms of liquidity events. Some fund managers have responded by facilitating structured secondary programs that give LPs liquidity options while maintaining GP control over the process. Others have resisted, viewing secondary activity as a vote of no confidence in their portfolio management.

Co-investment rights have become a significant bargaining chip in LP-GP negotiations. Large institutional LPs increasingly expect meaningful co-investment opportunities as a condition of their commitments, arguing that co-investments allow them to deploy capital more efficiently while building direct relationships with portfolio companies. For GPs, co-investment programs create administrative burden and can complicate financing rounds when multiple co-investors have varying preferences. The most successful partnerships have established clear frameworks for co-investment allocation that balance LP expectations with portfolio company needs.

Perhaps the most fundamental shift is occurring in how LPs evaluate GP performance. The traditional focus on IRR and multiple of invested capital is giving way to more nuanced frameworks that consider DPI (distributions to paid-in capital), portfolio construction strategies, and the sustainability of returns across market cycles. LPs who were burned by unrealized gains during the recent downturn are now placing greater emphasis on demonstrated ability to return capital rather than paper returns that may never materialize.

The implications of this reckoning extend beyond individual fund relationships to the structure of the venture industry itself. Emerging managers without established LP relationships face higher barriers to entry as institutional investors consolidate their allocations around proven managers. At the same time, mega-funds that raised during the peak of the cycle are finding subsequent fundraises more challenging as LPs question whether scale is compatible with venture-style returns. The middle ground—established managers with differentiated strategies and strong LP relationships—appears best positioned for the current environment, though even they must adapt to new expectations around transparency, alignment, and performance.
