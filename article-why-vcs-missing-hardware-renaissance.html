Opinion: Why Most VCs Are Missing the Hardware Renaissance

By Dr. Robert Chang • March 7, 2026 • 8 min read

A curious paradox has emerged in venture capital: at precisely the moment when hardware innovation is experiencing its most dynamic period in decades, most venture investors remain stubbornly focused on software. The reasons for this disconnect are understandable—software investment frameworks have proven enormously successful, and hardware's historical challenges with capital intensity, manufacturing complexity, and extended timelines have burned many funds. But this collective aversion is creating remarkable opportunities for investors willing to develop hardware-specific competencies.

The hardware landscape has fundamentally changed. Contract manufacturing capabilities have expanded dramatically, allowing startups to achieve production scale without building factories. Component costs for sensors, processors, and materials have declined along predictable curves. Design and simulation tools have reduced development cycles from years to months. The infrastructure that made hardware investment prohibitively risky a decade ago has been substantially de-risked by broader ecosystem development.

Meanwhile, the demand side has exploded. Climate technology requires physical infrastructure—batteries, solar installations, carbon capture systems, grid management hardware. Healthcare transformation depends on diagnostic devices, monitoring equipment, and therapeutic delivery systems. The AI revolution that VCs enthusiastically fund requires custom silicon, data center infrastructure, and edge computing hardware. Robotics and automation are entering their deployment phase across manufacturing, logistics, and agriculture. These are not speculative future markets; they are current, well-funded, rapidly growing sectors desperately seeking hardware solutions.

The investors who have developed hardware expertise are generating returns that challenge software-centric assumptions. Funds focused on climate hardware, defense technology, and advanced manufacturing have demonstrated that patient capital deployed with genuine technical understanding can produce venture-scale outcomes from physical products. These successes share common elements: deep technical diligence capabilities, longer time horizons baked into fund structures, and value-add that extends to manufacturing partnerships and supply chain optimization.

The opportunity cost of hardware aversion is becoming measurable. Software markets that attracted concentrated VC attention have become hypercompetitive, with crowded categories, compressed margins, and diminishing differentiation. Meanwhile, hardware categories with comparable or larger total addressable markets feature far fewer competitors for both deals and customers. The irony is that many investors have optimized for competition in markets where competition has eroded the returns they're optimizing for.

Building hardware investment capabilities requires genuine commitment. Surface-level interest—taking occasional hardware deals while maintaining software-centric evaluation frameworks—typically produces disappointing results. The most successful hardware investors have built specialized teams with manufacturing backgrounds, developed relationships with contract manufacturers and component suppliers, and structured funds with appropriate timelines and reserve ratios. This investment in capabilities creates barriers that protect returns as other investors eventually recognize hardware opportunities.

The next decade will likely see hardware returns that surprise the venture industry. The structural tailwinds are powerful: climate investment, reshoring initiatives, defense modernization, healthcare transformation, and AI infrastructure all require physical products. Investors who have built genuine hardware capabilities will capture disproportionate value. Those who remain committed to software-only strategies may find themselves optimizing for a market that has shifted beneath them.
