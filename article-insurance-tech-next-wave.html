The Next Wave of Insurance Tech: Beyond Distribution Plays

By Elizabeth Tanaka • February 17, 2026 • 7 min read

The first wave of insurtech largely focused on distribution—making it easier for consumers to buy insurance through digital channels, compare prices, and manage policies via mobile apps. This approach produced some notable successes but also revealed limitations. Digital distribution alone didn't fundamentally change insurance economics, and many early insurtechs found themselves competing on marketing spend rather than genuine product differentiation. The next wave of insurance technology is taking a different approach, targeting the core functions that actually determine insurance profitability: underwriting, claims processing, and risk infrastructure.

Underwriting—the process of evaluating risk and pricing policies accordingly—has been remarkably resistant to technological transformation. Traditional underwriters rely on limited data sets and established actuarial models that haven't changed meaningfully in decades. New entrants are challenging this by incorporating alternative data sources: telematics for auto insurance, satellite imagery for property coverage, wearable device data for health and life products. These approaches enable more precise risk segmentation, potentially allowing insurers to price coverage more accurately and profitably while offering better rates to low-risk customers.

Claims processing represents another area ripe for transformation. Traditional claims handling is labor-intensive, slow, and often adversarial—creating poor customer experiences while consuming substantial operational expense. Technology-driven approaches are automating significant portions of the claims workflow, using computer vision to assess damage, natural language processing to extract information from documents, and machine learning to detect fraud. The most advanced systems can process straightforward claims in minutes rather than weeks, dramatically improving customer satisfaction while reducing operational costs.

The infrastructure layer of insurance—the systems that manage policy administration, regulatory compliance, and integration with reinsurance markets—has received less attention than customer-facing applications but may offer the most durable opportunities. Insurance carriers operate on legacy technology stacks that constrain their ability to launch new products, enter new markets, or respond to competitive threats. Startups building modern insurance infrastructure enable carriers to modernize without replacing their core systems entirely, creating sticky relationships with enterprise customers.

Embedded insurance represents a convergence of distribution innovation and infrastructure capability. Rather than competing for customer attention directly, embedded approaches integrate insurance offerings into other purchase experiences—travel insurance at checkout for airline tickets, rental coverage during equipment booking, warranty protection bundled with electronics purchases. This model requires both the infrastructure to support real-time policy issuance and the distribution relationships to access these purchasing moments.

The regulatory landscape for insurtech has matured significantly. Early startups often underestimated the complexity of insurance regulation, which varies by state and product type and requires substantial compliance infrastructure. Successful second-wave insurtechs have developed genuine regulatory expertise, either by building in-house capabilities or partnering with established carriers who provide regulatory cover. This professionalization has raised barriers to entry but also created more sustainable competitive positions for those who navigate the complexity successfully.

Climate change is creating both opportunity and challenge for insurance technology. Traditional risk models are proving inadequate for a world of increasing weather volatility, creating demand for new approaches to catastrophe modeling and risk pricing. At the same time, climate-related losses are straining the insurance industry's profitability and raising questions about the insurability of certain risks. Insurtechs that can help the industry adapt to these changing conditions—through better risk modeling, new product structures, or innovative approaches to risk transfer—may find particularly receptive markets for their capabilities.
