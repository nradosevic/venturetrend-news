Term Sheet Red Flags Every First-Time Founder Should Know

By Amanda Chen-Ramirez • March 3, 2026 • 8 min read

When you receive your first term sheet, the temptation is to focus on the headline numbers: valuation and amount raised. These figures feel concrete and comparable. They're what you'll tell friends and what might make the news. Yet experienced founders know that the provisions buried in the fine print—liquidation preferences, anti-dilution clauses, board composition, and protective provisions—often matter more for long-term outcomes than the numbers that dominate the conversation.

Liquidation preferences determine how proceeds get distributed when a company is sold or liquidated. A straightforward 1x non-participating preference means investors get their money back before founders see anything, but once that threshold is cleared, proceeds are split according to ownership. This is standard and fair. The red flags appear with participating preferences, which allow investors to get their money back AND participate in the remaining proceeds pro rata—effectively double-dipping. Multiple liquidation preferences (2x or 3x) can make modest exits worthless for founders even when investors profit handsomely.

Anti-dilution provisions protect investors when subsequent rounds occur at lower valuations than their investment. Full ratchet anti-dilution, which adjusts the investor's conversion price to match any lower future price, can be devastatingly dilutive to founders in down rounds. Weighted average anti-dilution, which adjusts based on the size and price of the new round, is more founder-friendly and has become the market standard. Understanding the difference—and negotiating for weighted average provisions—can save significant equity in challenging market conditions.

Board composition and voting rights shape how decisions get made as the company grows. Investor-controlled boards can remove founders, veto strategic decisions, and force outcomes that benefit investors over the company's long-term interests. First-time founders sometimes accept board compositions that seem reasonable at seed stage without considering how they'll evolve through subsequent rounds. The standard advice—maintain founder board control at least through Series A—exists because the founders who don't often regret it.

Protective provisions give investors veto rights over specific company actions regardless of board composition. These typically cover major transactions like fundraising, acquisitions, or changes to company structure. The list of protected actions can be reasonable or expansive, and the difference matters enormously. Overly broad protective provisions can create situations where investors can block reasonable business decisions simply because those decisions don't align with their fund timelines or portfolio strategies.

Information rights and investor involvement clauses are often overlooked during negotiation but shape the founder experience going forward. Requirements for detailed monthly reporting, observer rights at board meetings, and consultation requirements on major decisions create ongoing obligations that consume founder time and attention. These provisions can be appropriate for investors who genuinely add value, but they can also become burdensome when investors use information rights to micromanage or second-guess management decisions.

Pay-to-play provisions require existing investors to participate in future rounds to maintain their preferred rights—those who don't participate see their preferred shares convert to common. These provisions can be either protective or punitive depending on how they're structured. When reasonable, they ensure that investors who participate in difficult rounds maintain their positions while those who abandon companies face consequences. When aggressive, they can force investors to participate in rounds they don't believe in or face outsized penalties.

The most important advice for first-time founders negotiating term sheets is to find experienced counsel and talk to other founders who have worked with the same investors. Term sheets are negotiable, and the provisions that investors propose reflect their starting positions rather than immutable requirements. Understanding what's standard, what's aggressive, and what's genuinely unusual gives founders the knowledge they need to push back effectively while preserving the relationship goodwill that makes investor partnerships successful over the long term.
