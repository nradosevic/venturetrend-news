Opinion: Why Conviction Beats Consensus in Early-Stage Investing

By Marcus Delgado • March 8, 2026 • 7 min read

There is a fundamental tension at the heart of venture capital that most practitioners would rather not discuss openly. The industry is built on the premise of funding unconventional ideas—businesses that challenge existing paradigms and create new markets. Yet the practice of venture investing has become increasingly consensus-driven, with investors herding toward hot sectors, co-investing in the same deals, and chasing the same metrics that everyone else chases. This contradiction explains much of the industry's recent struggles with returns and points toward what successful investing will require going forward.

Consider the math of venture returns. The distribution of outcomes in early-stage investing follows a power law: a small number of investments generate the vast majority of returns, while most investments return little or nothing. This mathematical reality has profound implications for investment strategy. If returns are concentrated in outliers, then the goal of venture investing is not to avoid losses or to generate consistent moderate returns. The goal is to find and fund the outliers—the companies that will generate 50x or 100x returns and define the portfolio's performance.

Outlier companies, almost by definition, look different from the companies that generate ordinary returns. They often emerge from unfashionable sectors, pursue business models that experts dismiss, or are led by founders whose backgrounds don't match investor pattern recognition. At the moment of investment, these companies typically face significant skepticism from the broader market. If they didn't—if the opportunity were obvious to everyone—competition for the deal would drive valuations to levels that make venture-scale returns impossible.

This is where conviction becomes essential. Investing in genuinely contrarian opportunities requires the willingness to be wrong in ways that will be visible to peers, LPs, and the broader market. It requires backing founders when other investors pass, maintaining conviction through difficult periods when the thesis appears to be failing, and continuing to support companies when the easy path would be to write them off. These behaviors are psychologically difficult and professionally risky. They are also the behaviors that generate exceptional returns.

The venture industry has developed structures that systematically discourage conviction. Partnership decision-making often defaults to consensus, allowing any partner to veto deals that others support. This process filters out controversial investments in favor of deals that everyone can agree on—precisely the deals most likely to be fairly priced and generate modest returns. Similarly, the pressure to deploy capital on regular timelines pushes investors toward investments that are available and consensus-approved rather than waiting for truly exceptional opportunities.

The proliferation of venture firms and the professionalization of the industry have amplified these tendencies. When multiple firms compete for the same deals using similar evaluation frameworks, investment decisions converge around shared views of what constitutes a good opportunity. Social dynamics—the desire to be seen as smart by peers, the fear of missing out on deals that others are doing—further reinforce consensus behavior. The result is a market that efficiently prices conventional opportunities while systematically underpricing unconventional ones.

For investors seeking to generate meaningful returns in this environment, the path forward requires deliberate cultivation of conviction. This means developing genuine expertise in specific domains rather than being generalists who evaluate deals across all sectors. It means building relationships with founders before they are ready to fundraise, allowing for deeper understanding of the people and visions behind companies. Most importantly, it means having the courage to act on conviction even when—especially when—that conviction contradicts market consensus. The returns in venture capital have always gone to those willing to be different. In an industry that has become increasingly similar, that willingness matters more than ever.
