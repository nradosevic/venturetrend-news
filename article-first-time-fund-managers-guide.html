What First-Time Fund Managers Need to Know About LP Expectations in 2026

By Rebecca Morrison • March 9, 2026 • 8 min read

The fundraising environment for emerging venture managers has shifted dramatically over the past two years. Limited partners who once eagerly backed promising first-time fund managers are now exercising substantially more caution, driven by a combination of denominator effect pressures, unrealized losses in existing venture portfolios, and a general pullback in risk appetite across asset classes. For aspiring fund managers, understanding this new landscape is essential to constructing a viable fundraising strategy.

The most fundamental shift involves track record expectations. While LPs have always valued demonstrated investing success, the bar for what constitutes a compelling track record has risen considerably. Angel investing portfolios that might have sufficed three years ago now require more substantial evidence of returns. LPs increasingly want to see not just successful company selections but demonstrated ability to win competitive deals, add value post-investment, and construct coherent portfolios—skills that predict success as a fund manager rather than just an individual investor.

Institutional LPs have also become more sophisticated in evaluating attribution. When emerging managers present track records from previous fund roles, LPs now dig deeper into the specific contributions that led to successful outcomes. Did the manager source the deal, lead the diligence, drive the decision, or simply participate in a partnership decision? This distinction matters enormously in predicting future performance, and managers who cannot clearly articulate their specific contributions face skepticism.

Fund economics have come under increased scrutiny as well. LPs are pushing back on management fee structures that don't align with realistic deployment timelines and fund sizes. First-time managers raising $50 million funds with 2% management fees face questions about whether $1 million annually truly supports sustainable operations or whether fee pressure will create misaligned incentives. Many emerging managers are accepting modified economics—lower management fees offset by founder-friendly carry structures—to demonstrate alignment with LP interests.

The most successful first-time fundraises share several characteristics. They typically feature managers with highly differentiated sourcing advantages—deep networks in specific geographies, technical domains, or founder communities that translate into proprietary deal flow. These managers can articulate exactly why they will see deals that established funds miss and why founders will choose them over better-known alternatives.

Operational planning has become a meaningful differentiator. LPs want to understand how emerging managers will build sustainable investment practices: who will handle operations, how will deal flow be managed, what systems will support portfolio monitoring. The most compelling emerging manager pitches include thoughtful operational plans that demonstrate the manager has considered the business-building aspects of fund management, not just the investing aspects.

Perhaps most importantly, patience has become essential. First-time fundraises that closed quickly during the 2020-2021 era now routinely take 18 to 24 months. Managers who demonstrate resilience through extended fundraising periods, continuing to build track record and relationships while seeking capital, often emerge with stronger foundations than those who achieved faster closes. The process itself has become a filter, and LPs are increasingly valuing the determination and resourcefulness that successful long fundraises demonstrate.
