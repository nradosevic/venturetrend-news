RegTech Is Having Its Moment: How Compliance Became a Growth Market

By Thomas Muller • February 20, 2026 • 7 min read

Compliance has traditionally been viewed as a cost center—something financial institutions had to do, not something they wanted to do. But as regulatory complexity has exploded and the costs of non-compliance have escalated, a new category of technology companies is transforming compliance from burden into competitive advantage. Regulatory technology, or RegTech, has emerged as one of the most compelling investment themes in financial services.

The market dynamics are compelling. Global regulatory requirements have proliferated dramatically since the 2008 financial crisis, with thousands of regulatory changes occurring annually across jurisdictions. Meanwhile, enforcement has intensified—financial institutions have paid hundreds of billions in fines over the past decade for compliance failures ranging from anti-money laundering violations to sanctions breaches. And as fintech companies proliferate, they create new compliance requirements for both themselves and the traditional institutions they work with.

Traditional compliance infrastructure simply can't keep pace. Banks and financial institutions have historically relied on armies of compliance officers manually reviewing transactions, policies, and documentation. This approach is expensive, error-prone, and inherently unscalable. As transaction volumes grow and regulations multiply, manual processes become untenable. The math no longer works—compliance costs can consume 10-20% of operating budgets at some institutions.

RegTech companies are attacking this problem from multiple angles. Some focus on know-your-customer and anti-money laundering, using AI to identify suspicious patterns that human reviewers would miss. Others automate regulatory reporting, translating complex requirements into standardized workflows. Identity verification, fraud detection, sanctions screening, and regulatory change management all represent distinct categories with specialized startups building focused solutions.

What makes RegTech particularly attractive from an investment perspective is the combination of recurring revenue and regulatory moats. Compliance solutions become deeply embedded in customer operations—ripping them out creates risk that few institutions are willing to accept. Customer retention rates exceed what most B2B software categories achieve. And the regulatory environment creates ongoing demand: as rules change, customers need their systems updated, creating natural expansion opportunities.

The market is evolving rapidly. Early RegTech companies often sold point solutions addressing specific compliance requirements. The current generation is building more comprehensive platforms that address multiple compliance needs through unified infrastructure. This platform approach creates stronger competitive positioning but requires more capital and longer development timelines. Investors must evaluate whether companies have the resources to execute on ambitious platform visions.

Challenges remain. Regulatory fragmentation across jurisdictions makes scaling geographically complex. Sales cycles to financial institutions can stretch to years, requiring substantial upfront investment before revenue materializes. And competition is intensifying as the market opportunity attracts more entrants—both startups and established software companies extending into compliance.

For investors, the key questions are identifying which specific compliance problems represent sustainable market opportunities, which companies have built defensible technology and go-to-market advantages, and whether current valuations appropriately reflect both the opportunity and the challenges. The category has matured significantly, but winners are still emerging, and the overall market has substantial room to grow as regulatory complexity shows no signs of abating.
